The AI Bubble: Are We Repeating History

AI is everywhere right now. New tools launch daily, companies add AI to every product name, and investors pour money into anything that sounds like machine learning. That hype raises a big question: are we in an AI bubble, and if yes, what happens when it pops?

What People Mean By ‚ÄúBubble‚Äù

A bubble happens when expectations grow faster than reality. Funding, confidence, and hype rise because everyone believes the future is guaranteed. Real progress exists at first, but once the story becomes bigger than results, people start paying for dreams instead of value.

Why AI Feels Like A Bubble

The biggest reason is speed. AI products move fast, so the hype cycle moves even faster. Many startups promise to replace entire jobs or automate full workflows. In practice, most tools still need humans to verify, edit, and guide them. That gap between marketing and everyday use creates ‚Äúbubble energy‚Äù.

Another reason is cost. Training large models requires expensive compute, and running them at scale also costs money. Some products grow quickly but struggle to become profitable, especially if usage depends on funding more than revenue.

But AI Is Also Real

Not every hype wave is meaningless. The internet had a bubble too, and many early companies failed, but the internet still changed the world. AI is similar: even if the market overheats, the technology will not disappear. It is already useful for writing, summarizing, translation, tutoring, and coding help.

What A Pop Might Look Like

If a pop happens, investors become stricter, weaker startups shut down, and companies stop adding AI everywhere just to sound modern. The loudest promises fade first. The quieter, useful products survive. That kind of correction can be healthy because it forces focus on real value.

How To Avoid Getting Tricked By Hype

Ask what problem the AI solves and whether people would pay for it without the buzzword. Strong products have a clear answer. Weak ones stay vague. The best AI is not magic‚Äîit‚Äôs a tool that saves time and reduces boring tasks.

My Take

AI has bubble energy right now, but that does not mean AI is a scam. It means excitement is ahead of reality. Some companies will fail and valuations will drop, but the real winners will be the ones building AI that is reliable, affordable, and genuinely useful in everyday life.

Passkeys vs Passwords

Passwords are easy to guess, reuse, leak, and phish. Even strong passwords can be stolen if a website gets breached or if you enter them into a fake login page. The problem is not only weak passwords, it is that passwords are a shared secret that can be copied.

What is a passkey

A passkey is a modern login method based on public key cryptography. Your device keeps a private key that never leaves the phone or computer. The website only gets a public key. When you log in, the site asks your device to prove it owns the private key, usually after Face ID, fingerprint, or a device PIN.

Why passkeys reduce phishing

Passkeys are tied to the real website domain. If you land on a fake site, the passkey will not authenticate there because the domain does not match. That removes the most common attack where users are tricked into typing a password.

What changes for users

You stop memorizing secrets. You unlock your device and you are in. The tradeoff is you must protect your device and use good account recovery options. The direction is clear though: more apps and platforms are moving toward passkeys because they improve security and also make login simpler.

What Is Zero Trust Security

For years, security was built like a castle: strong walls at the perimeter, and once you are inside the network, you are trusted. The problem is that modern attacks do not respect the perimeter. People work remotely, apps run in the cloud, and one stolen credential can move an attacker deep inside a company.

The core idea

Zero Trust means ‚Äúnever trust, always verify‚Äù. Every request must prove who it is, what it is allowed to access, and whether the context is safe. Being ‚Äúinside the network‚Äù is not enough anymore.

What it looks like in practice

You see multi factor authentication, device checks, least privilege access, and micro segmentation. Instead of one big internal network, systems are separated. If one account is compromised, the attacker cannot freely move to everything else.

Why it matters

Zero Trust is not a single tool, it is a strategy. It reduces the damage of breaches by limiting access and forcing verification at every step. In a world of phishing, leaked passwords, and cloud services, it is one of the most important security ideas to understand.
